When Knitting Meets Home Lab Life: My Unexpected Tech Break

Sometimes the best debugging happens away from the keyboard. How I discovered that knitting needles and network cables have more in common than you'd think.
Sun, Mar 8, 2026 · 4 min · Olivia Snowden

You know, after spending countless hours debugging Docker containers and wrestling with Kubernetes configurations, I never thought I’d find myself writing about… knitting. But here we are, and honestly, it’s been one of the most unexpectedly therapeutic additions to my home lab lifestyle. Who knew that after a day of troubleshooting network topologies, the rhythmic clicking of knitting needles could be exactly what my brain needed?

It started as a joke, really. My partner kept suggesting I needed a hobby that didn’t involve screens, and after the third time my Proxmox cluster went down during a weekend, I figured maybe they had a point. Fast forward six months, and my fiber optic cables now share shelf space with yarn skeins. The precision required for both is surprisingly similar.

My first knitting project was ambitious, to say the least. While most beginners start with scarves, I dove headfirst into colorwork sweaters. There’s something about following complex patterns that appeals to the same part of my brain that enjoys writing Ansible playbooks. Both require attention to detail, logical sequencing, and the occasional moment where you realize you’ve been doing everything backwards for the past hour.

The cable knit sweater was where things got really interesting. Working with cables in knitting is oddly reminiscent of managing actual network cables - there’s crossing, twisting, and keeping track of which strand goes where. The main difference is that when you mess up a knitting cable, you just have to rip back a few rows instead of crawling under your server rack with a flashlight.

Hats became my quick weekend projects, perfect for when I needed something to occupy my hands during long system updates or backup processes. There’s something deeply satisfying about completing a colorwork hat in the time it takes for a full system backup to finish. Plus, they make excellent gifts for fellow home lab enthusiasts who spend too much time in cold server rooms.

The purple ghost sweater represents what I call my “Halloween deployment season.” Just like how we have maintenance windows and deployment schedules in IT, apparently I have seasonal knitting themes. The colorwork technique here uses the same logical thinking as routing protocols - you have to plan your path, manage your resources (yarn colors), and troubleshoot when things don’t line up correctly.

The cardigan was my attempt at something more practical for the home office. Working from home means I’m constantly adjusting layers based on whether the servers are running hot or if I’ve got the AC cranked up. A button-up cardigan gives me the flexibility I need, plus the ribbing pattern is surprisingly meditative when you’re thinking through complex infrastructure problems.

What I’ve discovered is that knitting and system administration actually complement each other beautifully. Both require patience, problem-solving skills, and the ability to methodically work through complex processes. When I’m stuck on a particularly stubborn configuration issue, taking a break to work on a few rows of knitting often helps my brain process the problem in the background.

The repetitive nature of knitting stitches is like running a well-optimized script - once you get into the rhythm, your hands know what to do while your mind can wander and make connections. Some of my best infrastructure insights have come while working on stockinette stitch at 2 AM.

Plus, there’s something to be said for creating something tangible with your hands when your day job involves mostly virtual resources. After spending hours managing containers that exist only in memory and CPU cycles, producing an actual, physical sweater feels remarkably grounding.

So if you’re looking for a way to decompress between deployments, consider picking up some needles and yarn. Your servers will still be there when you get back, and you might just find that your problem-solving skills improve along with your cable knitting technique.

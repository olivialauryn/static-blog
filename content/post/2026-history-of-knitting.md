Knitting has been around way longer than you might think. Here's a quick rundown of how we got from ancient yarn to your favorite cozy sweater.

## The Early Days

The oldest known knitted artifacts date back to around the 11th century in Egypt — mostly socks, because apparently keeping your feet warm has always been a priority. These early pieces used a technique called *nalbinding*, which is a bit different from modern knitting but close enough to count.

## Knitting Goes Pro

By the 13th and 14th centuries, knitting guilds had popped up across Europe. These were serious operations — apprentices spent years mastering the craft before they could call themselves knitters. The trade was so lucrative that it was largely dominated by men for centuries. Wild, right?

## A Stitch in Every Home

The hand-knitting frame was invented in 1589 by William Lee, an English clergyman. Queen Elizabeth I reportedly turned him down when he asked for a patent because she didn't want to put hand-knitters out of work. Tough crowd.

By the 1800s, knitting had shifted from a professional trade to a domestic skill, mostly practiced by women at home. It became deeply tied to wartime efforts too — during both World Wars, knitting socks and scarves for soldiers was considered a patriotic duty.

## Knitting Today

Fast forward to now and knitting is having a serious moment. It's been reclaimed as a mindful, creative hobby with a huge online community (shoutout to Ravelry). Whether you're into chunky blankets or intricate lace shawls, there's a corner of the knitting world for you.

Not bad for a skill that's been around for a thousand years.
